Trajectory Following (Christian)

The trajectory following in our final project is based heavily on our pure pursuit algorithm from Lab 6. As such, the pure pursuit algorithm we made uses the following high level structure:

  • Update the Pose of the Robot: Once we receive a trajectory, we then initialize a subscriber which listens to an odometry topic and updates the pose of the robot.
  • Determine the Closest Point Each Line Segment: By parameterizing an equation for each line segment in the path, we find the closest point on each line segment to the current robot pose. From these distances, we find the closest line segment to the car.
  • Find the Goal Point: We find the intersection of a circle with radius equal to the lookahead distance and the line segments on the path to find a goal point to use as a steering heading. To prevent the robot from following the wrong path at intersections, we implemented a “segment lookahead” parameter, an integer that specifies how many segments the robot should look at in front or behind the closest segment.
  • Calculate the Steering Angle: The lookahead goal point is used to determine the angle used in Ackerman steering control, and the car drives towards the goal point until new trajectory segments are used. A proportional gain is applied to this steering angle to better the performance while turning corners on the trajectory.

Figure 2: The diagram above outlines the high-level concept governing the steering angle for pure pursuit.

An important addition to our pure pursuit algorithm for the final race is the inclusion of variable speed. Our controller determines the distance from the pose of the car to the start and the end of the closest segment. If these distances are above certain thresholds, it means that the car is in a long straightaway, and can afford to accelerate. The deceleration threshold is quite large, as it is more important to take a curve without crashing than it is to take the curve quickly. The acceleration threshold is smaller, but still large enough to ensure the car has corrected its orientation and is pointed along the trajectory before it speeds up.

Safety Avoidance Mechanism (S.A.M.) (Ethan)

The first few implementations of Integration and Trajectory planning had worked well initially, but would occasionally crash into obstacles or become a bit off course. In running and testing the first stages of integration and trajectory planning, it became evident that our approach needed some variable fine tuning or an additional component. Due to a lack of hardware equipment to run the simulations on, fine tuning the variables for our trajectory was done and an additional add-on was worked on in parallel after viewing inconsistent results. This addition became, “Smart Avoidance”.

Previously, Operation Pure Pursuit from Lab6 was completed and had worked well. However, our out-sourced localization code in combination with Pure Pursuit led to a build up of errors that caused our autonomous vehicles that would cause our vehicle to crash into obstacles near our previously tuned path of trajectories. This was to be expected as the aforementioned worked on only attempting to follow the path without taking into consideration any of its surroundings, leading for the need of a complimentary piece that only considered the vehicles nearby surroundings for real-time obstacle avoidance.

Abstractly, if the vehicle were to experience a future collision, it would cause an emergency turn towards a relative left or right direction. In deciding whether to turn left or right, the vehicle considers a quick estimation of which direction appears to be more promising. It does this by comparing the closest set of points near itself in either direction and chooses the one with more room to turn towards. Due to limited testing capacity, there was a distance forward from the car between a set of angles between, “Theta 1” and “Theta 2” respectively. These values were fine tuned after observing its function through simulation. Although a more complex version would have been pursued where these angles were variable to the car’s speed, and only the distance directly in front of the car would be considered (of where the car would be).  The car’s max turning speed of 30 degrees would have also been considered for non-collision avoidance when considering the vehicle’s possible turning radius. (Rather than all of this being estimated through observation rather than calculation)

Trajectory Planning (Christian)

Using the Python and Matlab tools mentioned above, we were able to quickly make and modify trajectories for the final project, streamlining our efforts to tune the performance of our car during the challenge. The trajectories shown below were plotted in Matlab in a way that allowed us to drag points to better locations when necessary.

Figure 5: The leftmost trajectory is our first attempt, which was  built by driving the car around the track and manually noting positions. The middle trajectory is our local solution, which was built by dragging segment points to better locations. The rightmost trajectory is our final trajectory, which was made by modifying our local solution such that the sharper corners account for differences in computation speed between our local machines and the test server.

Our initial trajectory had very round corners, which resulted in the car turning too early cutting corners, and ultimately not finishing the race. Because our car uses a high lookahead distance, curving the corners had the effect of shifting the lookahead point in the direction of turning before it should be shifted. This problem was remedied by making the majority of corners quite sharp (with the notable exception of the first corner, which was left rounded to account for the high speed of the car at the start of the turn). As shown in Figure 5, our local solution was quite simple, used sharp corners, and performed well. Running on our local machines without a safety controller, a car following this trajectory completed the course in 59 seconds and had two minor collisions (see Trajectory Following). Finally, our competition trajectory is a version of our local solution in which the corners are sharper (to account for some unknown difference between our local system and the competition test system). Running on the competition system with the addition of a safety controller, a car following this trajectory completed the course in 58.95 seconds and had one light and one brush collision (see Safety Avoidance Mechanism). At the end of the project, we determined that a well made trajectory is necessary, but time should be spent optimizing a safety controller rather than fine tuning the locations of each segment endpoint in the trajectory.
